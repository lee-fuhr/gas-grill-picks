How to choose a gas grills

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Focus on burner count, cooking area, build quality, and BTUs, but don’t chase the highest number. A well-built three-burner grill often outperforms a flimsy four-burner model. Choose based on how many people you typically cook for and what features matter most for your style of grilling.

How many burners do you really need?

The question most buyers start with is whether to go with three or four burners. Three-burner grills are the sweet spot for most households cooking for two to six people. They offer enough firepower to sear steaks on one side while indirect-cooking a chicken on the other. Four-burner grills add that extra zone, which helps when you need to keep a large batch of burgers or vegetables at different temperatures at the same time. If you host big parties or love cooking multiple dishes, say, flank steak on high, shrimp skewers on medium, and bell peppers on low, four burners give you the flexibility. But for everyday meals, a solid three-burner model with a reliable side burner for sauces will feel like plenty. Don’t let the count alone decide; the quality of the burners and the spacing matter just as much.

What to look for in grill construction and materials

A propane grill is an outdoor appliance that lives through rain, sun, and temperature swings. The material of the firebox, lid, and cart makes the difference between a grill that rusts in two seasons and one that stays sturdy for years. Look for heavy-gauge stainless steel or powder-coated steel for the body; cast aluminum is also excellent for corrosion resistance and heat retention. The cooking grates are the part your food touches. Porcelain-coated cast iron grates hold heat well and give those restaurant-quality sear marks. Stainless steel grates are easier to clean but don’t sear quite as aggressively. Avoid thin, chrome-plated wire grates, they heat unevenly and don’t last. Don’t overlook the cart and wheels. A wobbly frame is dangerous when you’re rolling the grill or when it’s loaded with a hot rotisserie assembly. Check that the wheels are large enough to handle your deck or patio surface.

BTUs and heat output: what matters and what doesn’t

Manufacturers often tout high BTU (British Thermal Unit) numbers as a sign of more power. But BTUs alone don’t tell you how hot the grill gets or how evenly it cooks. A small, poorly insulated grill with a high BTU burner can blast intense heat in one spot and leave the rest cold. A well-designed grill with moderate BTUs and good heat distribution often outperforms a cheap high-BTU model. What really matters is the BTU per square inch of primary cooking area. A reasonable range is about 80 to 100 BTUs per square inch. Also pay attention to the burner design: U-shaped or H-shaped burners provide more even coverage than straight tubes. Heat tents (flavorizers) help vaporize drippings and spread heat. In short, don’t buy the grill with the biggest number on the box. Look for a combination of solid construction, efficient burner layout, and thoughtful heat management.

Which extra features are worth the money?

Many grills come with side burners, rotisserie kits, built-in thermometers, and storage cabinets. A side burner can be genuinely useful for warming sauces or sautéing onions while you grill. But if you have a stove inside a few steps away, you might never use it. The same goes for rotisserie: excellent for whole chickens and roasts, but only if you’ll actually buy the spit and assemble it. A built-in lid thermometer is handy but often inaccurate. You’re better off using an instant-read digital thermometer for meat. Look for sturdy side shelves that fold down to save space when not in use. An enclosed cart with doors protects your propane tank and provides dry storage for tools. Don’t pay extra for a sear station or infrared burner unless you do a lot of high-heat searing of thick steaks. For most buyers, a well-built standard grill with decent burners will produce excellent results.

Propane vs natural gas: which fuel makes sense for you?

The large majority of portable and mid-sized grills run on propane from a refillable tank. Propane is easy to transport, widely available, and gives intense heat right away. The downside is tank management, you need to keep a spare or refill before a cookout. Natural gas grills connect to your home’s gas line, so you never run out of fuel mid-burger. But natural gas burns at a lower pressure, so you lose some heat output, and you’re stuck with the grill in one spot. If you’re planning a permanent outdoor kitchen or have a gas hookup already, natural gas is convenient. For most people buying a grill for a patio or deck, propane is the simpler, more flexible choice. Many grills come with a conversion kit, but not all are officially certified for natural gas conversion. Check the manufacturer’s compatibility before you buy.

Frequently asked questions

How many BTUs do I need for a propane grill?

Instead of looking at total BTUs, aim for about 80 to 100 BTUs per square inch of primary cooking area. A typical three-burner grill has roughly 400 to 550 square inches, so you’d want between 32,000 and 55,000 total BTUs from a well-designed burner system.

Should I get a three-burner or four-burner grill?

A three-burner grill is enough for most families cooking for two to six people. A four-burner grill gives you more temperature zones and is better for larger groups or cooking multiple dishes with different heat levels at the same time. Base your decision on how many people you typically feed and your cooking style.

What is the best material for cooking grates on a propane grill?

Porcelain-coated cast iron grates offer the best heat retention and searing ability, though they require a little more care to prevent chipping. Stainless steel grates are easier to clean and rust-resistant but don’t sear as sharply. Avoid thin chrome-plated wire grates that wear out quickly.

Can I convert a propane grill to natural gas?

Many grills offer conversion kits, but not all are officially safety-certified for natural gas conversion. Check the manufacturer’s specifications and use only approved conversion parts. Be aware that natural gas produces slightly lower heat output than propane, so your grill’s maximum temperature may drop.

How long does a standard propane tank last on a grill?

A typical 20-pound propane tank will fuel a three-burner grill on medium heat for about 18 to 22 hours of total cooking time. High-heat searing and cold weather burn through propane faster. It’s smart to keep a spare tank so you don’t run out mid-cookout.

What size grill fits my patio or deck?

Measure your available space, including clearance for the lid to open fully and room for the propane tank on the side. A three-burner grill is usually about 48 to 55 inches wide, while a four-burner grill can be 55 to 65 inches. Leave at least 10 inches behind the grill for venting and safety.

In shortThe best propane grill for you is the one that fits your cooking frequency, party size, and budget without chasing flashy numbers. Focus on solid build, even heat distribution, and a burner count that matches your typical menu. A three-burner grill with quality materials and smart features will serve most homes well for years, while a four-burner model is worth the extra cost if you regularly cook for a crowd or want precise zone control. Keep it simple, keep it sturdy, and you’ll never regret the choice.
