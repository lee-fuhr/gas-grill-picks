Gas Grills questions, answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

For most households, a 3-burner propane gas grill offers plenty of cooking space for burgers, steaks, and veggies for a family of four to six. A 4-burner model gives you extra room and more precise heat zones, ideal for larger gatherings or those who like to cook multiple dishes at once. Focus on build quality, burner layout, and grate material rather than just BTU count.

How many burners do I need?

The number of burners directly relates to total cooking area and heat control. A 3-burner grill typically provides around four hundred to five hundred square inches of primary cooking space, which comfortably handles a dozen burgers or a couple of large steaks. That’s enough for a family of four to six, with room for a few sides. A 4-burner grill jumps to roughly five hundred to seven hundred square inches, giving you the flexibility to cook for eight to ten people without juggling. The extra burner also creates a dedicated low-heat zone, useful for indirect cooking of roasts or whole chickens. If you regularly host parties or cook large cuts of meat, a 4-burner is a worthwhile step up.

3 burner vs 4 burner: what’s the real difference?

Beyond cooking capacity, the main difference is heat versatility. A 3-burner grill gives you left, center, and right zones, which works fine for most direct grilling. A 4-burner lets you set up two separate temperature zones on one side and a cooler zone on the other, making it easier to sear on high heat while gently cooking something else. Price and size also climb. A 4-burner grill is generally heavier, requires more patio space, and costs a few hundred dollars more than a comparable 3-burner. Both types are available in portable and stationary models, but 4-burners are almost always full-size carts. Consider your typical cookout size and whether you value extra zone control over saving space and money.

What features matter most on a propane grill?

Start with build quality. Look for a stainless steel or heavy-gauge steel firebox and lid; thin metal can warp and rust quickly. Burners should be stainless steel or covered in a durable coating, tube burners are common and reliable. The grate material is equally important: cast iron holds heat best but needs seasoning, while porcelain-coated grates are easier to clean but can chip. An electronic ignition that lights consistently is a must. Many grills now include a built-in lid thermometer, side shelves for prep, and a grease management system. BTUs are often overhyped; more important is how evenly the heat distributes across the grates. A good mid-range grill with four hundred to five hundred BTUs per burner is more than sufficient for searing.

How much should you spend on a good 3 or 4 burner grill?

You can find budget-friendly 3-burner grills that perform well for occasional use, but expect thinner steel and less precise temperature control. Mid-range models, which cost a few hundred to several hundred dollars, offer better burners, sturdier construction, and more consistent heat. Premium grills, running into the thousands, add heavy-gauge stainless, infrared searing, and rotisserie systems. For a 4-burner, the starting point is higher. A decent mid-range 4-burner will run you about fifty percent more than a comparable 3-burner. But you don’t need to spend top dollar: many solid 4-burner options from well-known brands hit the sweet spot between durability and price. Focus on warranty length and owner reviews to gauge long-term value.

What size grill fits your patio?

Measure your available space before shopping. A typical 3-burner cart grill is about forty-eight to fifty-six inches wide when the side shelves are folded. A 4-burner cart is often fifty-eight to sixty-six inches wide, and that’s not counting the space you need for safe clearance from walls or railings. If you have a small balcony or tight patio, consider a 3-burner model with fold-down shelves or a compact portable design. For a dedicated grilling station, a 4-burner with a side burner and enclosed cabinet can be a permanent addition. Also think about storage: many grills come with doors or drawers for propane tanks and accessories.

How to get the best performance from your gas grill?

Preheat with the lid closed for ten to fifteen minutes to get the grates hot enough for a good sear. Clean the grates with a brass brush after each use to prevent buildup, and deep-clean the burners and grease tray every few months. For indirect cooking on a 3-burner, turn off the center burner and place food over it; on a 4-burner, leave the two middle burners off and cook over the cooler zone. Wind can affect flame stability, so position your grill out of strong drafts. If you find hot spots, rotate food or move it to a cooler area. A quick visual check of the flame colour, it should be blue with a yellow tip, tells you the air mixture is correct. If the flame is mostly yellow, clean the burner ports.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use a 3-burner grill for a large family?

Yes, a 3-burner grill is fine for a family of four to six. If you regularly cook for eight or more, a 4-burner gives you more surface area and better heat management for larger batches.

Do I need a rotisserie kit?

A rotisserie is nice for whole chickens, roasts, and kebabs, but it’s not essential. Many grills offer an aftermarket kit that mounts on the burner bars. If you love slow-roasted meats, look for a model with a built-in rotisserie motor mount.

What is the best material for grill grates?

Cast iron grates offer the best heat retention and searing, but require seasoning and regular maintenance to prevent rust. Porcelain-coated grates are easier to clean and lighter, but can chip. Stainless steel grates resist rust and last long, but don’t hold heat as well as cast iron.

How often should I clean my grill?

Brush the grates after each use while they’re warm. Deep-clean the burner tubes and grease tray every few months, or more often if you use it heavily. A clean grill prevents flare-ups and extends the life of the burners and firebox.

Is propane cheaper than charcoal?

Propane is generally more expensive per cookout than charcoal, especially if you buy pre-filled tanks. However, the convenience of instant heat, easier cleanup, and consistent temperature control makes it a trade-off many prefer. Large propane tanks can reduce per-use cost if you refill them.

Should I get a grill with a side burner?

A side burner is useful for sautéing onions, melting butter, or keeping a sauce warm without going inside. It adds about fifteen to thirty percent to the grill’s footprint. If you often cook entire meals outdoors, it’s a nice addition; otherwise, you can skip it to save space and cost.

In shortWhichever burner count you choose, the best grill for you is one that fits your cooking style, your patio, and your budget. A well-built 3-burner will serve most households beautifully, while a 4-burner offers extra capacity and versatility for bigger gatherings. Keep the build quality, grate material, and even heat distribution at the top of your checklist, and you’ll be set for years of great grilling.
